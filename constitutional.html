Important Concepts from Constitutional Law

1. Preamble

The Preamble is the introduction to the Constitution which states its objectives: Justice, Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity. It declares India to be a Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic.

2. Fundamental Rights (Part III)

Fundamental Rights are basic rights guaranteed to every citizen. Major rights include:

  • Article 14: Right to Equality
  • Article 15: Prohibition of discrimination
  • Article 19: Freedom of speech, assembly, association, movement, residence, and profession
  • Article 21: Protection of life and personal liberty
  • Article 21A: Right to Education
  • Article 32: Right to Constitutional Remedies

3. Directive Principles of State Policy (Part IV)

These are guidelines for the state to ensure social and economic democracy:

  • Equal pay for equal work
  • Provision for just and humane conditions of work
  • Promotion of education and public health
  • Uniform Civil Code for all citizens

4. Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)

Added by the 42nd Amendment, these duties include respecting the Constitution, promoting harmony, safeguarding public property, and protecting the environment.

5. Union Executive

Includes the President, Vice-President, Prime Minister, and Council of Ministers.

  • Article 52: President of India
  • Article 63: Vice-President of India
  • Article 74: Council of Ministers

6. Parliament (Articles 79-122)

Includes Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, and the President. Powers include law-making, amending Constitution, approving budgets, etc.

7. Judiciary

Supreme Court: Highest court (Articles 124-147) — interprets the Constitution and protects fundamental rights.
High Courts: Function under Articles 214-231.
Judicial Review: Courts can strike down unconstitutional laws.

8. Emergency Provisions

  • Article 352: National Emergency (due to war/external aggression)
  • Article 356: President's Rule (failure of constitutional machinery)
  • Article 360: Financial Emergency

9. Amendment of the Constitution

Article 368 provides for amending the Constitution. Some parts require only Parliament’s approval, others need ratification by half of the states.

10. Schedules in the Constitution

There are 12 schedules. Some important ones are:

  • Schedule 1: Names of States and Union Territories
  • Schedule 7: Union, State, and Concurrent Lists
  • Schedule 8: Languages
  • Schedule 10: Anti-defection law

11. Important Constitutional Cases

  • Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala: Established the Basic Structure Doctrine
  • Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India: Expanded interpretation of Article 21
  • Golak Nath v. State of Punjab: Limited Parliament’s power to amend Fundamental Rights
  • Minerva Mills v. Union of India: Reaffirmed balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles
