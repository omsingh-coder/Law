Naz Foundation v. Government of NCT of Delhi (2009)

Background

The Naz Foundation, an NGO working on HIV/AIDS and sexual health, filed a Public Interest Litigation in the Delhi High Court in 2001 challenging the constitutional validity of Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code (IPC), which criminalized "carnal intercourse against the order of nature," effectively criminalizing homosexual acts even between consenting adults.

The petitioner argued that Section 377 violated fundamental rights guaranteed under the Indian Constitution, especially those related to dignity, privacy, and non-discrimination.

Key Legal Issues

  • Does Section 377 violate the right to equality (Article 14)?
  • Does it infringe upon the right to freedom (Article 19)?
  • Does it violate the right to life and personal liberty (Article 21)?
  • Is criminalization of consensual homosexual acts a breach of human dignity and privacy?

Arguments

Naz Foundation: Argued that Section 377 marginalized the LGBTQ+ community, violated their right to privacy and dignity, and hindered HIV/AIDS prevention efforts.

Government (initially): Opposed the petition but later the Union Government left it to the wisdom of the court.

Judgment

In a historic judgment delivered on 2nd July 2009, the Delhi High Court read down Section 377 and held:

  • Section 377 is unconstitutional insofar as it criminalizes consensual sexual acts between adults in private.
  • Violation of Article 21: It infringes the right to dignity, privacy, and health.
  • Violation of Article 14: The provision is arbitrary and unfair toward a specific class of people.
  • Violation of Article 15: Discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation is not permitted.

Significance

- First Indian judgment to decriminalize homosexuality.
- Recognized sexual orientation as a ground of non-discrimination.
- Marked a shift in Indian jurisprudence toward inclusion and human rights.
- Strengthened the LGBTQ+ movement and public awareness.

Aftermath

- The judgment was overturned by the Supreme Court in Suresh Kumar Koushal v. Naz Foundation (2013).
- However, it laid the foundation for the final decriminalization in Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018), where the Supreme Court unanimously struck down Section 377.

Quote from the Judgment

"If there is one constitutional tenet that can be said to be underlying the Constitution, it is that of inclusiveness... Indian Constitution reflects this value deeply ingrained in Indian society."
