Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

Background

In 1977, Maneka Gandhi, a journalist and daughter-in-law of former Prime Minister Indira Gandhi, was issued a passport under the Passport Act, 1967. Within a week, she received a notice from the Regional Passport Office asking her to surrender the passport. No reason was cited except that it was in the "public interest".

When Maneka Gandhi requested the reason for the withdrawal, the authorities refused to disclose it. She filed a writ petition under Article 32 of the Constitution directly in the Supreme Court, alleging that the government’s order violated her Fundamental Rights, particularly under Article 14, 19(1)(a), 19(1)(g), and Article 21.

Key Issues

  • Does the "right to travel abroad" fall within the scope of Article 21?
  • Can personal liberty be curtailed by a procedure that is arbitrary or unjust?
  • Does Article 21 include the requirement of a fair and reasonable procedure?
  • Should Articles 14, 19, and 21 be read together or separately?

Arguments

Petitioner (Maneka Gandhi):
The restriction was arbitrary and denied without affording her an opportunity of being heard. It violated the principles of natural justice and procedural fairness.

Respondent (Union of India):
Claimed that the government had powers under the Passport Act to impound a passport in public interest and was not obligated to disclose reasons in every case.

Judgment

The seven-judge constitutional bench delivered a unanimous verdict in favor of Maneka Gandhi. The Court held that:

  • Right to travel abroad is part of personal liberty under Article 21.
  • Any law affecting personal liberty must pass the test of reasonableness and must not be arbitrary.
  • "Procedure established by law" under Article 21 must be just, fair, and reasonable—not fanciful, oppressive, or arbitrary.

Golden Triangle Doctrine

The Court observed that Articles 14, 19, and 21 are interlinked and cannot be treated as watertight compartments. Any law interfering with life and liberty must also be tested against equality under Article 14 and freedom under Article 19.

Highlights of the Case

  • Overruled the narrow interpretation of Article 21 given in A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950).
  • Expanded the scope of Article 21 to include dignity, privacy, right to shelter, and education.
  • Emphasized the importance of procedural fairness and natural justice.
  • Established that fundamental rights must be interpreted liberally.

Impact

This case transformed the interpretation of Article 21 and opened the door to a broader reading of fundamental rights. It became the basis for future judgments on the right to privacy, right to shelter, and right to live with dignity.

The doctrine that emerged from this case laid the foundation for a progressive, rights-based jurisprudence in India.

Quote from Judgment: "No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to a procedure established by law – which must be fair, just and reasonable, not arbitrary or oppressive."
