Mohd. Ahmed Khan v. Shah Bano Begum (1985)

Background

Shah Bano, a 62-year-old Muslim woman and mother of five, was divorced by her husband, Mohd. Ahmed Khan, in 1978. After the divorce, she filed a petition under Section 125 of the Criminal Procedure Code (CrPC), seeking maintenance.

Her husband argued that under Muslim Personal Law, he was only required to provide maintenance during the iddat period (roughly 3 months after divorce) and was not liable thereafter. This case eventually reached the Supreme Court.

Legal Issues

  • Can a divorced Muslim woman claim maintenance under Section 125 of CrPC?
  • Does personal law override the general law applicable to all citizens?
  • Is the husband liable to maintain his wife beyond the iddat period?

Arguments

Shah Bano's Counsel: Argued that Section 125 of CrPC applies to all citizens irrespective of religion, and the husband's duty to maintain his divorced wife exists if she cannot maintain herself.

Husband’s Counsel: Argued that under Muslim law, liability ends after iddat. Thus, general law (CrPC) shouldn’t apply to Muslims in personal matters.

Judgment

The Supreme Court, in a landmark decision, ruled in favor of Shah Bano:

  • Section 125 of CrPC applies to all citizens regardless of religion.
  • The husband is liable to provide maintenance even after the iddat period if the wife is unable to maintain herself.
  • The Court observed that personal laws cannot override constitutional rights and obligations under general law.

Significance

- Strong affirmation of gender justice and secular legal principles.
- Sparked a national debate on the Uniform Civil Code (Article 44 of the Constitution).
- Became a flashpoint in Hindu-Muslim personal law discussions.

Aftermath

Facing political pressure from orthodox sections, the then government passed the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986 which diluted the effect of the Shah Bano judgment by limiting maintenance rights under Muslim personal law.

However, subsequent judgments and amendments clarified that Muslim women can still claim maintenance under CrPC if needed.

Quote from the Judgment

"There is no conflict between Section 125 and Muslim Personal Law. Section 125 is a secular provision applicable to all."
